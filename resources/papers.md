# Important Papers in Quantum Mechanics and Computing

Key research papers that shaped our understanding of quantum theory and quantum computing. Organized by topic and historical significance.

---

## Foundational Papers: Quantum Mechanics

### The Birth of Quantum Mechanics

**"On the Quantum Theory of Radiation" - Einstein (1916)**
- Introduced concept of stimulated emission
- Foundation for understanding photons
- Led to development of lasers

**"Quantization as an Eigenvalue Problem" - Schrödinger (1926)**
- Introduced wave equation for quantum mechanics
- Formulation still used today
- Alternative to Heisenberg's matrix mechanics

**"The Physical Principles of the Quantum Theory" - Heisenberg (1930)**
- Matrix mechanics formulation
- Uncertainty principle
- Complementarity

**"The Principles of Quantum Mechanics" - Dirac (1930)**
- Unified and generalized quantum mechanics
- Introduced bra-ket notation
- Relativistic quantum mechanics

## Quantum Entanglement and Non-Locality

### The EPR Paradox

**"Can Quantum-Mechanical Description of Physical Reality Be Considered Complete?" - Einstein, Podolsky, Rosen (1935)**
- The famous EPR paper
- Argued quantum mechanics is incomplete
- Introduced concepts later called entanglement
- **Why read:** Understand the paradox that led to Bell's theorem
- **Citation:** Phys. Rev. 47, 777 (1935)

### Bell's Theorem

**"On the Einstein Podolsky Rosen Paradox" - John S. Bell (1964)**
- Proved local hidden variables incompatible with quantum mechanics
- Derived Bell inequalities
- Made EPR debate experimentally testable
- **Why read:** THE most important paper on quantum foundations
- **Citation:** Physics Physique Физика 1, 195 (1964)

**"Proposed Experiment to Test Local Hidden-Variable Theories" - Clauser, Horne, Shimony, Holt (1969)**
- Made Bell's ideas experimentally practical
- CHSH inequality
- Foundation for all Bell tests
- **Citation:** Phys. Rev. Lett. 23, 880 (1969)

### Experimental Tests

**"Experimental Test of Bell's Inequalities Using Time-Varying Analyzers" - Aspect, Dalibard, Roger (1982)**
- First convincing experimental test
- Closed important loopholes
- Confirmed quantum mechanics
- **Citation:** Phys. Rev. Lett. 49, 1804 (1982)

**"Loophole-free Bell inequality violation" - Multiple groups (2015)**
- Hensen et al., Giustina et al., Shalm et al.
- Final confirmation of quantum mechanics
- All major loopholes closed
- **Citation:** Various, Nature and PRL

## Quantum Information Theory

### Foundational Papers

**"Communication Theory of Secrecy Systems" - Shannon (1949)**
- Not quantum, but foundation for information theory
- One-time pad security
- **Why read:** Understand classical information theory first

**"A Mathematical Theory of Communication" - Shannon (1948)**
- Birth of information theory
- Entropy, channel capacity
- Foundation for quantum information

**"Information is Physical" - Landauer (1991)**
- Connections between information and physics
- Erasure costs energy
- **Citation:** Physics Today 44(5), 23 (1991)

**"Quantum Theory, the Church-Turing Principle and the Universal Quantum Computer" - Deutsch (1985)**
- Defined quantum Turing machine
- Universal quantum computation
- Foundation of quantum computing
- **Citation:** Proc. R. Soc. Lond. A 400, 97 (1985)

## Quantum Computing: Algorithms

### Early Algorithms

**"Quantum Theory, the Church-Turing Principle and the Universal Quantum Computer" - Deutsch (1985)**
- First quantum algorithm (Deutsch's algorithm)
- Proved quantum speedup possible
- **Citation:** Proc. R. Soc. Lond. A 400, 97 (1985)

**"Rapid Solution of Problems by Quantum Computation" - Deutsch & Jozsa (1992)**
- Deutsch-Jozsa algorithm
- Exponential speedup for specific problem
- **Citation:** Proc. R. Soc. Lond. A 439, 553 (1992)

### Shor's Algorithm

**"Algorithms for Quantum Computation: Discrete Logarithms and Factoring" - Shor (1994)**
- THE paper that made quantum computing famous
- Polynomial-time factoring algorithm
- Threatens RSA encryption
- **Why read:** Most important quantum algorithm
- **Citation:** Proceedings of FOCS 1994

**"Polynomial-Time Algorithms for Prime Factorization and Discrete Logarithms on a Quantum Computer" - Shor (1997)**
- Journal version with more details
- **Citation:** SIAM J. Comput. 26(5), 1484 (1997)

### Grover's Algorithm

**"A Fast Quantum Mechanical Algorithm for Database Search" - Grover (1996)**
- Quadratic speedup for search
- Provably optimal
- Widely applicable
- **Citation:** Proceedings of STOC 1996

**"Quantum Mechanics Helps in Searching for a Needle in a Haystack" - Grover (1997)**
- Extended explanation
- **Citation:** Phys. Rev. Lett. 79, 325 (1997)

### Quantum Walks

**"Quantum Random Walks" - Aharonov, Davidovich, Zagury (1993)**
- Introduced quantum walks
- Foundation for many algorithms
- **Citation:** Phys. Rev. A 48, 1687 (1993)

## Quantum Error Correction

### Foundational Papers

**"Scheme for Reducing Decoherence in Quantum Computer Memory" - Shor (1995)**
- First quantum error correcting code
- Showed quantum error correction is possible
- **Why read:** Overcame major objection to quantum computing
- **Citation:** Phys. Rev. A 52, R2493 (1995)

**"Error Correcting Codes in Quantum Theory" - Steane (1996)**
- CSS codes (Calderbank-Shor-Steane)
- Elegant construction
- **Citation:** Phys. Rev. Lett. 77, 793 (1996)

**"Quantum Error Correction via Codes over GF(4)" - Calderbank, Rains, Shor, Sloane (1998)**
- Mathematical framework for quantum codes
- **Citation:** IEEE Trans. Inf. Theory 44, 1369 (1998)

### Threshold Theorems

**"Fault-Tolerant Quantum Computation with Constant Error Rate" - Aharonov & Ben-Or (1997)**
- Proved fault-tolerant QC possible
- Error threshold exists
- **Citation:** STOC 1997

**"Resilient Quantum Computation" - Preskill (1997)**
- Threshold theorem
- Scalability of quantum computing
- **Citation:** Proc. R. Soc. Lond. A 454, 385 (1998)

## No-Go Theorems

**"A Single Quantum Cannot be Cloned" - Wootters & Zurek (1982)**
- No-cloning theorem
- Fundamental limitation
- Basis for quantum cryptography security
- **Citation:** Nature 299, 802 (1982)

**"Communication by EPR Devices" - Eberhard (1978)**
- Cannot signal faster than light using entanglement
- Important for causality
- **Citation:** Nuovo Cimento B 46, 392 (1978)

**"Constraints on Quantum Computation" - Various authors**
- Various no-go results
- Important for understanding limitations

## Quantum Cryptography

**"Quantum Cryptography: Public Key Distribution and Coin Tossing" - Bennett & Brassard (1984)**
- BB84 protocol
- First quantum key distribution
- Provably secure
- **Citation:** Proc. IEEE Int. Conf. on Computers, Systems and Signal Processing

**"Quantum Cryptography Using Any Two Nonorthogonal States" - Bennett (1992)**
- B92 protocol
- Simplified QKD
- **Citation:** Phys. Rev. Lett. 68, 3121 (1992)

**"Quantum Key Distribution Over 100 km of Standard Fiber" - Various groups (1990s-2000s)**
- Practical implementations
- Long-distance QKD

## Quantum Teleportation

**"Teleporting an Unknown Quantum State via Dual Classical and Einstein-Podolsky-Rosen Channels" - Bennett et al. (1993)**
- Introduced quantum teleportation
- Uses entanglement + classical communication
- **Why read:** Beautiful application of quantum mechanics
- **Citation:** Phys. Rev. Lett. 70, 1895 (1993)

**"Experimental Quantum Teleportation" - Bouwmeester et al. (1997)**
- First experimental demonstration
- **Citation:** Nature 390, 575 (1997)

## Reading Papers Effectively

### For Beginners

1. **Start with abstracts:** Understand main claim
2. **Read introduction:** Context and motivation
3. **Look at figures:** Often tell the story
4. **Skim conclusions:** What was achieved
5. **Skip heavy math initially:** Come back later

### Building Understanding

1. **Find review papers first:** Get overview
2. **Read original papers:** After understanding context
3. **Follow citations:** Build knowledge tree
4. **Take notes:** Summarize in own words
5. **Discuss:** Forums, study groups

### Critical Reading

1. **What's the claim?** Be clear on what's proven
2. **What are assumptions?** Often hidden
3. **Is proof rigorous?** Or heuristic argument?
4. **Experimental support?** Theory vs. demonstration
5. **What remains open?** Papers solve some problems, raise others
